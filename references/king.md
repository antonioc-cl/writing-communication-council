# Stephen King

## Core Philosophy
Stephen King conceives writing as an almost magical act: *"writing is telepathy"*. For King, putting words on a page allows transferring ideas, images, and feelings directly from the writer's mind to the reader's, through time and space. Additionally, he argues that *"writing is refined thinking"*, because the drafting process forces clarification and concrete shaping of ideas that would otherwise remain nebulous. In his vision, the blank page is a place of pure communication between minds, where words are the vehicle of that creative telepathy.

Another cornerstone of his philosophy is that **to write well you must read a lot and write a lot**. King insists on daily discipline: he doesn't believe in capricious muses, but in constant habit. He recommends reading voraciously to soak up vocabulary, styles, and structures, and writing every day to improve the craft. There are no shortcuts or magic formulas; progress comes with continuous practice. His approach is "just write": overcoming procrastination and perfectionism through constant work.

Finally, King defends **emotional truth**. Stories work when they are honest, even uncomfortable. Fear — of criticism, ridicule, failure — is the writer's greatest enemy. Writing well means saying the emotional truth of the text and trusting that the reader will recognize it.

## Key Frameworks

### The Toolbox
**King's perspective:** Writing is a craft skill. Vocabulary, grammar, and style are tools, not adornments.
**Application to non-fiction:** Clarity, precision, and language mastery generate credibility in emails, documents, and proposals.
**Valid outside fiction:** Without technical foundations, there's no good communication.

### Situation over Plot
**King's perspective:** The best stories are born from a powerful situation, not a rigid outline.
**Application to business:** Start from the real problem or context, let the text organize around that.
**Valid outside fiction:** Less artificial structure, more organic logic.

### Description as Character
**King's perspective:** Description reveals character and emotion.
**Application to business:** Concrete examples and real details make cases and arguments credible.
**Valid outside fiction:** The specific connects more than the abstract.

### Adverbs Are Not Your Friend
**King's perspective:** Adverbs betray insecurity and weaken the text.
**Application to business:** Eliminate "very", "really", "extremely". Use precise words.
**Valid outside fiction:** Less filler, more power.

### The Ideal Reader
**King's perspective:** Write for a specific person.
**Application to business:** Visualize your target reader when writing.
**Valid outside fiction:** Empathy and focus improve any text.

### First Draft with the Door Closed
**King's perspective:** The first draft is private and uncensored.
**Application to business:** Separate creation from editing.
**Valid outside fiction:** Improves flow and reduces block.

## Signature Tactics

1. **Realistic dialogue**
   Rule: write like people speak.
   Example: incomplete phrases, fillers.
   Business: conversational tone.

2. **Create tension**
   Rule: pose questions, delay answers.
   Example: cliffhangers.
   Business: open with a real problem.

3. **Kill your darlings**
   Rule: cut what doesn't serve.
   Example: remove beautiful but useless scenes.
   Business: focus on the message.

4. **Daily routine**
   Rule: write every day.
   Example: 2000 daily words.
   Business: fixed writing blocks.

5. **Second draft**
   Rule: review with distance.
   Example: let the text rest.
   Business: re-read with fresh eyes.

6. **The 10% rule**
   Rule: cut 10%.
   Example: condense chapters.
   Business: shorter emails.

7. **Read as a writer**
   Rule: analyze what you read.
   Example: study rhythm and structure.
   Business: learn from good texts in the field.
